INTRODUCTION

Welcome. ,

This audiobook covers five cutting-edge research papers on cold fusion — also called Low Energy Nuclear Reactions, or LENR. ,

You don't need a physics PhD for this. By the end, you'll understand what these scientists are doing, why it matters, and what they actually found. ,

Let's start with the basics, then dive into each paper.

PART ONE: WHAT IS COLD FUSION — AND WHY DOES IT MATTER?

Normal nuclear fusion — the kind that powers the sun — works by slamming two hydrogen nuclei together so hard they fuse into helium and release massive energy. ,

The problem? You need temperatures of tens of millions of degrees to do that. That's why "hot fusion" reactors are enormous, expensive, and still not commercially viable after seventy years of trying. ,

In 1989, two electrochemists named Martin Fleischmann and Stanley Pons made a shocking announcement. They said they had achieved nuclear fusion at room temperature — in a jar on a lab bench. ,

The scientific world exploded. The press called it "cold fusion." Time magazine ran it as a cover story. ,

Then, almost immediately, it collapsed. Other labs couldn't reproduce the results consistently. Most mainstream scientists dismissed it as error or fraud. ,

But here's what almost nobody knows: a small, determined international community of researchers never stopped. ,

They rebranded the field. Instead of "cold fusion," they started calling it LENR — Low Energy Nuclear Reactions — or LANR — Lattice Assisted Nuclear Reactions. ,

They published in peer-reviewed journals, ran conferences, and slowly, carefully, built up evidence. ,

The journal you're listening to right now — the Journal of Condensed Matter Nuclear Science, Volume 40, published in November 2025 — is the latest collection of that work. ,

It contains five papers. Each one investigates a different angle of LENR. ,

Let's go through them one by one.

PART TWO: PAPER ONE — MAKING PALLADIUM RELEASE HEAT WITH LASER LIGHT

Title: "On the Exothermic Response Frequencies When PdD Is Subjected to Coherent THz Radiation"
Author: J. S. Brown

The Setup

Palladium is a silvery-white metal. You've probably heard of it in catalytic converters. It has a remarkable property: it can absorb hydrogen — or its heavier twin, deuterium — like a sponge. ,

Deuterium is hydrogen with an extra neutron. Normal hydrogen has one proton and zero neutrons. Deuterium has one proton and one neutron. It's roughly twice as heavy. It's also the fuel of choice in fusion experiments. ,

When palladium absorbs deuterium until every available slot is filled, chemists write it as PdD — palladium deuteride. ,

Physicists Dennis Letts and Peter Hagelstein discovered something remarkable. When they shone infrared laser light at PdD at very specific frequencies — in the terahertz range, which sits between microwave and visible light — the material released anomalous heat. ,

Not just any heat. Excess heat. More heat than the laser was putting in. ,

Three frequencies triggered this effect: roughly 8.3, 15.1, and 20.5 terahertz. ,

The Q-values — a measure of how sharply tuned the response is — ranged from 14 to 39. That's a very narrow, precise resonance. It's not thermal noise. It's a real physical phenomenon. ,

Hagelstein proposed one explanation involving phonon bands — vibrations that propagate through a crystal lattice. But his model couldn't explain the 21-terahertz response. And Brown, the author of this paper, questions Hagelstein's approach on fundamental grounds. ,

Brown's Explanation

Brown argues the frequencies match something else entirely: the vibrational energy levels of individual deuterium nuclei — called deuterons — trapped inside the palladium crystal. ,

Here's the picture. Inside PdD, each deuteron sits in a tiny cage called an octahedral site — a symmetrical pocket surrounded by six palladium atoms. ,

The deuteron isn't glued in place. It vibrates. And because it's a quantum particle, it can only vibrate at specific, quantized energy levels — like rungs on a ladder. ,

Brown calculated the energy levels of these trapped deuterons using quantum mechanics, solving the equations on a three-dimensional grid with a pitch of 0.25 angstroms — that's 0.25 ten-billionths of a meter. ,

He found specific transitions — jumps between energy levels — that correspond almost exactly to the three laser frequencies that trigger heat release in the experiments. ,

The matches: the observed 8.3 THz corresponds to his calculated 9.8 THz transition. The 15.1 THz matches his 15.7 THz. The 20.5 THz matches his 21.1 THz. ,

And he made a prediction: there should be a fourth response at 18.0 THz that Hagelstein's curve fitting overlooked. When Brown checked the raw experimental data table — rather than the fitted curve — there was indeed a response at exactly 18.0 THz. ,

The Collective Twist — Tavis-Cummings

This is where it gets really interesting. Brown doesn't just explain why certain frequencies matter. He proposes a mechanism for how this could lead to fusion. ,

When laser radiation is intense enough, it doesn't just wiggle one deuteron. It locks all the deuterons in the surface layer into a collective quantum state. They all vibrate in sync. ,

This is modeled by something called the Tavis-Cummings Hamiltonian — a mathematical framework developed for quantum optics that describes how a single photon mode couples to a large ensemble of identical two-level systems. ,

Think of it like this: normally each deuteron is doing its own thing, like musicians in a room playing random notes. The laser is like a conductor who brings them all into perfect synchrony — one giant collective orchestra. ,

In this coherent state, called the Dicke ground state, the effective interaction between neighboring deuterons is enhanced. Brown speculates that this enhancement could amplify the normally negligible deuteron-deuteron fusion rate by a factor equal to the number of deuteron neighbors. ,

In the surface layer of PdD, the laser only penetrates about one micron deep — one millionth of a meter. But within that layer, N deuterons all act as one. ,

When fusion does occur in this coherent state, the 24 million electron-volt energy release can't escape as a single gamma ray — because the wavelength of such a gamma ray is far smaller than the coherence volume. Instead, the energy dissipates as many low-energy phonons — lattice vibrations — which is heat. ,

This is potentially why cold fusion doesn't produce lethal radiation. The energy comes out as heat, not as high-energy particles. ,

Key takeaway: Brown matched the laser frequencies that trigger anomalous heat in PdD to specific quantum energy levels of trapped deuterons. He then proposed that laser-induced collective quantum coherence could amplify fusion rates. It's theoretical, but it's the most complete explanation anyone has offered.

PART THREE: PAPER TWO — TRIGGERING FUSION WITH ALPHA PARTICLES

Title: "Random Thermal Spikes Observed in 99% Palladium – 1% Uranium Alloy Foil Electrolytically Charged with Deuterium: Confirmation of Solid-State Fusion"
Authors: Douglas A. Pinnow and Kirk Miller

The Big Idea

Pinnow and Miller had a hypothesis: what if Fleischmann and Pons failed to be consistently reproduced because they were missing a trigger? ,

Nuclear reactions almost always need an initiating event. What if deuterium fusion in a palladium lattice needs a spark — a tiny, intensely energetic event to get things started? ,

Their candidate: alpha particles. ,

Alpha particles are helium nuclei — two protons and two neutrons — ejected spontaneously by certain radioactive elements. Uranium-238, the main component of depleted uranium, is one such emitter. ,

Here's the physics: when a uranium atom decays and fires an alpha particle, all that energy — several million electron volts — deposits into an incredibly tiny volume. The local temperature spikes to something comparable to the interior of the sun. ,

This is called a thermal spike. It's been known since the 1950s. But Pinnow recognized it as a potential fusion trigger. ,

The Experiment

They fabricated small foils of 99% palladium mixed with 1% depleted uranium — essentially traces of uranium-238 evenly distributed through a palladium matrix. ,

Then they ran a classic Fleischmann-Pons style electrolytic experiment. They immersed the foil in heavy water — water made with deuterium instead of normal hydrogen — and ran an electrical current through it. ,

Electrolysis splits the heavy water and drives deuterium ions into the palladium. After about two hours of loading, they consistently observed temperature spikes in the foil, detected by thermocouples — temperature sensors attached to the foil. ,

The spikes were random in location across the foil surface, and random in time — exactly what you'd expect from spontaneous radioactive decay events. ,

The Controls

This is important. They ran four types of experiments to rule out alternative explanations. ,

First, normal water with palladium-uranium foil: no spikes. Second, heavy water with pure palladium foil (no uranium): no spikes. Third, heavy water with palladium-uranium: spikes. ,

Both the deuterium and the radioactive trigger were required. Remove either one, and the effect disappears. ,

They also calculated the magnitude of the spikes. A single alpha decay or a single deuterium-deuterium fusion event couldn't produce the temperature change they observed. ,

Their conclusion: billions of fusion reactions occurred in a tight cluster — a chain reaction. ,

The D-D fusion reaction most relevant here is: D plus D yields tritium plus a proton, releasing 4.03 million electron volts. This energy is locally deposited, raising the temperature further, potentially triggering more fusion events in the surrounding deuterium-loaded palladium. ,

They found they could control the power output by adjusting the electrolytic loading current — the rate at which deuterium is driven into the foil. More current, stronger effect. Less current, weaker effect. This controllability is critical for any practical energy device. ,

They explicitly frame this as solid-state fusion — SSF — to contrast it with plasma fusion, the tokamak-style approach that dominates mainstream fusion research. ,

Key takeaway: Pinnow and Miller showed that adding alpha-particle emitters to palladium gives a consistent, controllable trigger for fusion. This may be why early replication attempts of Fleischmann-Pons failed — they didn't include a trigger.

PART FOUR: PAPER THREE — THERMODYNAMICS AND COLD FUSION

Title: "Thermodynamics in Lattice Assisted Nuclear Reactions"
Author: Mitchell R. Swartz, ScD, EE, MD

Why Thermodynamics?

This paper takes a step back and asks: what does classical physics — specifically thermodynamics — tell us about LENR systems? ,

Thermodynamics is the science of energy and its transformations. It's why engines work, why heat flows from hot to cold, why you can't build a perpetual motion machine. ,

Swartz argues that you cannot do good LENR science without understanding thermodynamics deeply. It governs your electrodes, your calorimetry measurements, and your emissions. ,

He walks through all four laws of thermodynamics and shows how each one applies — or doesn't — to cold fusion systems. ,

The Zeroth Law

The Zeroth Law says: if two objects are in thermal contact long enough, they reach the same temperature. Simple. It's what makes thermometers work. It's what makes calorimetry possible. ,

The First Law — Conservation of Energy

The First Law says energy is conserved. You can't create it from nothing. ,

Swartz traces this law back to its origin story — Dr. Julius Robert Mayer, a 19th-century German physician who noticed that patients' blood was a different color in tropical Java versus Europe. He reasoned that less oxygen was extracted in the heat, which led him to connect heat, work, and energy conservation. ,

This law is the bedrock of LENR calorimetry. When an experiment claims excess heat, it must satisfy the First Law. Energy in must equal energy out — unless there's an additional source, like nuclear reactions. ,

Swartz discusses Bremsstrahlung — the X-ray radiation that decelerating charged particles emit. In hot fusion, Bremsstrahlung is intense and penetrating — dangerous to humans. ,

In cold fusion, the temperatures are vastly lower. The skin depth of palladium in the infrared range — roughly 3 to 21 nanometers — traps most of the Bremsstrahlung inside the metal. It can only escape as blackbody radiation — heat — or as phonons. ,

This is why cold fusion doesn't irradiate researchers. The energy is locked in. The effective dose from cold fusion Bremsstrahlung compared to hot fusion drops by eleven to eighteen orders of magnitude. That's 10 to the power of 11 to 18 times less dangerous. ,

Swartz also carefully examines flow calorimetry — the method most commonly used to measure LENR energy output. He derives the Navier-Stokes equations for the calorimetric fluid flow and introduces a non-dimensional number he calls eta-B — the buoyancy factor. ,

If you ignore thermal buoyancy — the tendency of hot water to rise — your calorimetry can be seriously wrong. Swartz shows how to correct for this. Many failed replications of LENR may have suffered from this error. ,

The Second Law — Entropy

The Second Law says entropy — disorder — always increases in a closed system. Heat flows from hot to cold, never the reverse, spontaneously. ,

Swartz connects this to Shannon information theory. Entropy in physics and entropy in information theory are formally identical. Both measure disorder. Both constrain what processes can spontaneously occur. ,

LENR systems are open systems — they exchange energy and matter with their environment. Open systems can locally decrease entropy while the universe's total entropy still increases. This is how life exists. It's how LENR systems can organize hydrogen into coherent quantum states. ,

The Third Law — And Why It Breaks

The Third Law says entropy approaches zero as temperature approaches absolute zero in a perfect crystal. ,

Swartz presents a stunning counterexample: water itself. ,

Water has two lattices. The oxygen atoms form a perfectly ordered hexagonal lattice. But the hydrogen atoms — the protons — are disordered. They can sit in multiple equivalent positions across each oxygen bridge. ,

Swartz calculates the entropy of this proton disorder. Even at absolute zero, ice retains an entropy of R times the natural log of 1.5 — about 0.81 calories per gram-mole. The measured value is 0.82. ,

This non-zero entropy at absolute zero directly violates the Third Law as stated. ,

This matters for LENR because heavy water — deuterium oxide — shows similar disorder. And this disorder affects how LENR systems load deuterium and how they behave at different temperatures. ,

Key takeaway: Swartz provides the thermodynamic framework for understanding LENR measurements. Calorimetry must account for buoyancy effects. Bremsstrahlung in cold fusion is trapped inside the metal — making it safe. And the Third Law of Thermodynamics fails for water, which matters for any LENR system that uses water.

PART FIVE: PAPER FOUR — GRAPHENE AND CORONA DISCHARGE FOR CONTROLLED LENR

Title: "The Use of Graphene for Controlled LENR in Thin Conductive Targets via Optimized Low-Voltage Corona Discharge"
Authors: V. I. Vysotskii, M. V. Vysotskyy, and S. Bartalucci

A New Approach

Most LENR experiments use electrolysis — running electricity through heavy water to force deuterium into a metal. This paper tries something different: corona discharge. ,

A corona discharge is the electrical glow you sometimes see at the tips of power lines in foggy weather. It's a partial electrical breakdown of air — not a full spark, but a sustained region of ionized gas around a sharp conductor. ,

Vysotskii and colleagues propose using low-voltage corona discharge to drive hydrogen or deuterium ions into thin conductive targets. And they use graphene as the target material. ,

Why Graphene?

Graphene is a single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal honeycomb lattice. It is one atom thick — about 0.3 nanometers. It is incredibly strong, highly conductive, and transparent. ,

It is, by definition, a two-dimensional material. And LENR in two dimensions behaves very differently from LENR in three-dimensional bulk metals. ,

In a standard palladium experiment, fusion events — if they occur — are buried deep inside a three-dimensional lattice. Detection is difficult. Energy dissipates in all directions. ,

In a graphene layer, everything happens in one plane. The confinement changes the quantum mechanics. Particles interact differently in two dimensions. Wavefunctions are more concentrated. The probability of tunneling through the Coulomb barrier — the electromagnetic repulsion between two nuclei — can be significantly higher. ,

The Coulomb barrier is the fundamental obstacle to low-energy nuclear fusion. Two positively charged nuclei repel each other. To fuse, they must be pushed close enough together that the strong nuclear force takes over. Normally this requires enormous temperatures. ,

In a confined lattice — and especially in a two-dimensional lattice — quantum tunneling allows a small probability of nuclei getting close enough to fuse even at room temperature. The question is whether that probability is large enough to matter. ,

Coherent Correlated States

This paper draws heavily on a theoretical framework developed by Vysotskii and Vysotskyy over many years: coherent correlated states. ,

Standard quantum mechanics says the uncertainty in a particle's position times the uncertainty in its momentum must be at least ℏ divided by two — Planck's constant over two. This is Heisenberg's uncertainty principle. ,

But there's a generalization: for position and momentum, the product of uncertainties must exceed ℏ over two — but only if the position and momentum are uncorrelated. ,

If position and momentum are strongly correlated — which happens in certain dynamical systems, like rapidly oscillating potentials — the position uncertainty can become extremely large, while the momentum uncertainty becomes extremely small. The particle's wavefunction can spread out enough to significantly overlap with neighboring nuclei. ,

This is the coherent correlated state — a quantum state in which a nucleus's wavefunction is stretched to overlap with its neighbors, dramatically increasing the probability of fusion without needing high temperatures. ,

The corona discharge creates exactly the kind of rapidly oscillating, non-equilibrium field that can drive hydrogen ions into coherent correlated states within the graphene lattice. ,

The team proposes this as a highly controllable LENR platform: tunable voltage, tunable ion flux, and a well-characterized two-dimensional material. ,

Key takeaway: Graphene's two-dimensional confinement and corona discharge's controllable ion implantation combine to create a new, optimized LENR platform. The coherent correlated state theory provides the quantum mechanical basis for why confinement enhances fusion probability.

PART SIX: PAPER FIVE — SENDING FUSION ACROSS THE ROOM WITH HEAT WAVES

Title: "Stimulation of Efficient Low Energy Tritium Fusion Under The Action of a Weak Undamped Thermal Wave on Remote TiD Target"
Authors: Vladimir I. Vysotskii, Alla A. Kornilova, and Sergey N. Gaydamaka

The Most Surprising Paper

This paper describes something that sounds almost impossible: triggering nuclear fusion in a target that is physically separated from the energy source — at a distance of up to two meters — using special heat waves. ,

Not sound waves. Not electromagnetic waves. Heat waves. ,

Standard vs. Undamped Thermal Waves

Here is something you probably learned in a physics class: heat doesn't travel as waves the way sound or light does. Heat diffuses. It spreads out. A pulse of heat injected into a material decays exponentially within about one wavelength. ,

This is described by the classical heat equation — the Fourier diffusion equation. It predicts that heat waves damp out almost immediately. ,

But Vysotskii and colleagues identified a flaw in this classical theory. The standard Fourier heat equation assumes that temperature and heat flux change perfectly synchronously in any small volume. This is actually an approximation — and it breaks down when you consider relaxation times. ,

In any real material, there's a thermodynamic relaxation time — the time it takes for the atoms to reach thermal equilibrium after being disturbed. Call it tau. ,

When you include tau — when you add a time-delay correction to the heat equation — the mathematics changes dramatically. ,

The modified equation predicts that at specific frequencies — called resonant frequencies — thermal waves propagate without any attenuation. They don't damp out. They travel indefinitely. ,

The formula for these frequencies is omega-n equals (2n + 1) times pi divided by 2 times tau, where n is any non-negative integer. ,

For air at typical laboratory conditions, the lowest such frequency is about 80 megahertz. ,

These undamped waves have been experimentally detected at distances of up to 2 meters in laboratory settings. The detection limit wasn't a physical limit — it was just the size of the room. ,

Generating the Waves

The team generates undamped thermal waves using cavitation of a water jet. ,

Cavitation is the formation and collapse of bubbles in a liquid under rapid pressure changes. When high-pressure water — at 150 atmospheres — exits through a 2-millimeter steel channel into an enclosed chamber, bubbles form and collapse violently. ,

This violent collapse creates intense shock waves and soft X-rays — X-ray photons with energy around 1.5 kiloelectron volts. These X-rays are absorbed by the surrounding air, creating intense local heating pulses. ,

These pulses are brief — shorter than the relaxation time tau — so they contain spectral components at the resonant undamped frequencies. The result: undamped thermal waves propagate outward from the cavitation chamber. ,

The Remote Target

The target is a foil of titanium deuteride — titanium metal loaded with deuterium — denoted TiD. It sits at a distance from the cavitation source. ,

The undamped thermal waves hit the TiD target and deposit energy in a highly specific, frequency-selective way. The energy goes into the deuterium nuclei — the deuterons — within the titanium lattice. ,

This drives the deuterons into non-equilibrium states. And the team argues that in these states — specifically coherent correlated states — the fusion probability is dramatically enhanced. ,

What They Measured

They measured nuclear products using CR-39 plastic track detectors placed behind the TiD target. CR-39 is the same plastic used in eyeglass lenses. High-energy charged particles — like protons and helium nuclei — leave microscopic damage tracks that can be etched and counted under a microscope. ,

They looked for two fusion reactions: D plus D yields tritium plus a proton — the tritium-producing channel — and D plus D yields helium-3 plus a neutron. ,

The results were striking. The tritium production rate was approximately 100 million — ten to the eighth power — times higher than the helium-3 production rate. ,

This asymmetry is completely opposite to what hot fusion predicts. In hot fusion, both channels occur at nearly equal rates. ,

But in LENR, theory predicts exactly this kind of channel selection. Coherent correlated states favor the tritium-producing channel by many orders of magnitude. ,

The total fusion rate increased quadratically with irradiation time — a nonlinear response that suggests the waves aren't just enabling individual fusion events, but are progressively restructuring the titanium deuteride matrix — potentially opening nanocracks that further enhance LENR conditions. ,

The reliability of the results is supported by multiple controls: the track detectors showed no background in unexposed experiments, the total particle count scaled linearly with irradiation duration between 20 and 40 minutes, and the tracks from helium-3 nuclei showed perfect axial symmetry consistent with the geometry of the experiment. ,

Key takeaway: Undamped thermal waves — a consequence of relaxation corrections to standard heat equations — can travel long distances without attenuation and trigger nuclear fusion in remote deuterium-loaded targets. The tritium-to-helium-3 ratio is 100 million to one — confirming LENR channel selection distinct from hot fusion.

PART SEVEN: CONNECTING THE DOTS

You've now heard five papers. Let's zoom out and see the bigger picture. ,

All five papers share a common thread: quantum coherence. ,

In Brown's paper, it's the collective Dicke ground state of deuterons driven into phase coherence by laser light. ,

In Pinnow and Miller's paper, the alpha particle creates a microscopic thermal spike so intense it drives neighboring deuterons into unusual quantum states — enough to initiate a chain reaction. ,

In Swartz's thermodynamics paper, the quantum disorder in water's proton sublattice is fundamental to understanding LENR calorimetry. Calorimetry is how we measure the energy output of LENR. Get it wrong, and you dismiss real results. ,

In the graphene paper, two-dimensional confinement enables coherent correlated states where deuteron wavefunctions spread out enough to overlap with neighbors — dramatically enhancing tunneling. ,

And in the thermal wave paper, undamped heat waves selectively drive deuterons into coherent correlated states in a remote target — at room temperature — producing tritium at a rate 100 million times more efficient than the neutron-producing channel. ,

The emerging picture across all five papers: LENR is not about individual particles randomly tunneling through barriers. It's about collective quantum states — many nuclei acting in synchrony — in which the effective tunneling probability is dramatically enhanced. ,

PART EIGHT: WHY THIS FIELD MATTERS

Even if you remain skeptical — and healthy skepticism is appropriate — LENR deserves attention for several reasons. ,

First, the energy density. A deuterium-deuterium fusion reaction releases about 4 million electron volts. Chemical combustion of hydrogen releases about 2.5 electron volts. That's a factor of roughly 1.6 million. ,

If even a small fraction of the deuterium in seawater — which is essentially unlimited — could be fused via LENR, the energy implications are planetary in scale. ,

Second, the safety profile. Unlike hot fusion or fission, LENR — if the theoretical picture is correct — produces energy primarily as heat and phonons, with minimal high-energy radiation. No meltdown risk. No spent fuel rods. ,

Third, the commercial momentum. The editor's preface, written by Jean-Paul Biberian, notes that multiple startup companies are now working toward commercial LENR products. After more than 36 years of basic research, the field is moving toward engineering. ,

A successful commercial demonstrator, Biberian writes, will be the key to open the door to making cold fusion a reality. ,

PART NINE: SUMMARY OF ALL FIVE PAPERS

Let's do a final rapid-fire review. ,

Paper 1 — Brown: Three laser frequencies trigger anomalous heat in palladium deuteride. These frequencies match quantum energy level transitions of individual trapped deuterons. Collective quantum coherence — the Tavis-Cummings model — may amplify fusion. ,

Paper 2 — Pinnow and Miller: Alpha particles from uranium-238 trigger temperature spikes in deuterium-loaded palladium foil. Both the trigger and the fuel are required. Billions of fusion events occur in chain reactions. The effect is controllable. ,

Paper 3 — Swartz: Thermodynamics governs all LENR measurements. Calorimetry requires buoyancy corrections. Cold fusion Bremsstrahlung is safe — trapped in the metal. Water's proton disorder violates the Third Law, and this matters for LENR behavior. ,

Paper 4 — Vysotskii, Vysotskyy, Bartalucci: Graphene's two-dimensional structure combined with corona discharge creates a controlled, optimized LENR platform. Coherent correlated states explain enhanced tunneling probability in confined geometries. ,

Paper 5 — Vysotskii, Kornilova, Gaydamaka: Undamped thermal waves — predicted by relaxation-corrected heat equations — travel without attenuation and trigger LENR in remote titanium deuteride targets. Tritium production is 100 million times more efficient than neutron production, confirming LENR channel selection. ,

CLOSING

Cold fusion was declared dead in 1989. Thirty-six years later, researchers around the world are publishing peer-reviewed evidence of anomalous heat, chain reactions, selective nuclear channels, and remote stimulation of fusion using heat waves. ,

The theoretical frameworks have matured: quantum coherence, Tavis-Cummings models, coherent correlated states, undamped thermal waves. ,

The experimental evidence has grown: calorimetry, track detectors, controlled triggers, reproducible results. ,

Is LENR real? The honest answer is: probably something real is happening, but the exact mechanism remains debated. ,

What's certain is that the scientists publishing in this journal are doing careful, thoughtful work — using quantum mechanics, thermodynamics, and nuclear physics at the frontier of our understanding. ,

Whatever you decide to believe, the question they're asking is one of the most important in science: can we unlock nuclear energy at room temperature? ,

If the answer is yes, it changes everything. ,
